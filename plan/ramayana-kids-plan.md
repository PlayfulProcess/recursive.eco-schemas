## Vision

The Ramayana retold as devotional-cosmic mythology for children. Rama isn't just a prince — he's Vishnu the Preserver choosing to forget his divinity and experience human limitation, loss, and love. Sita isn't a passive victim — she's Lakshmi incarnate and Earth goddess, born from the furrow, returning to the earth when she's done. Hanuman isn't just a monkey — he's bhakti made flesh, devotion so total it grants the power to leap oceans and carry mountains. Ravana isn't just a villain — he's a ten-headed genius who mastered the cosmos but couldn't master his own desire. Lanka isn't just a fortress — it's the ego's golden palace, beautiful and impregnable until love breaks it open.

Esoteric sources:
- **Rudolf Steiner** — Hindu spiritual evolution as the third post-Atlantean epoch; Vishnu's avatars as stages of consciousness descent
- **Joseph Campbell** — The hero's journey mapped onto Rama's exile-quest-return; Hanuman as the supernatural helper
- **Tulsidas** — Ramcharitmanas (16th century devotional version) — the bhakti lens, Hanuman as perfect devotee
- **Valmiki** — The original Ramayana — the more complex, morally ambiguous source text
- **Southeast Asian traditions** — Javanese/Balinese shadow puppets (wayang), Thai Ramakien, Cambodian Reamker — the Ramayana as living, performed mythology across cultures
- **Wendy Doniger** — The tension between dharma and compassion in the Rama story

## Moral Complexity Notes

The Ramayana contains scenes that require careful handling:

1. **Vali's killing**: Rama shoots Vali from behind a tree during his duel with Sugriva. This is the most debated scene in all of Indian literature. The L1 verse tells the story straight; the Whisper acknowledges the moral tension; the L2 Thread engages with the dharma debate.

2. **Sita's fire trial (Agni Pariksha)**: After rescue, Sita must walk through fire to "prove her purity." The L1 verse tells it as "the fire that REFUSED to burn her" (emphasizing Sita's power, not her subjection). The Whisper acknowledges the patriarchal framing and offers the reading that Sita is demonstrating her power over the elements, not submitting to judgment.

3. **Sita's exile (Uttara Kanda)**: Rama banishes pregnant Sita based on a washerman's gossip. The L1 `return-sita-earth` tells this as Sita CHOOSING to return to the earth — she calls her mother (the Earth) and the ground opens to receive her. The Whisper engages with the tension between Rama as ideal king (dharma) and Rama as failed husband (love).

4. **Surpanakha's mutilation**: Lakshmana cuts off her nose and ears. The L1 verse can focus on the confrontation without graphic detail. The Whisper notes that this act of cruelty is what triggers the entire abduction plotline — violence breeds violence.

## Illustration Sources

### Primary: Raja Ravi Varma — Ramayana Paintings (1890s)
- **~30 oleographs/paintings**: Lush, colorful, iconic depictions of all major scenes
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons, public domain
- **Style:** Academic realism meets Indian mythological tradition — the defining visual language of modern Hindu iconography
- **Coverage:** Birth, swayamvara, exile, Sita's abduction, Hanuman, battle, return

### Secondary: Balasaheb Pant Pratinidhi — Ramayana Paintings (1916)
- **~50 paintings**: Rich color, detailed scenes, less idealized than Ravi Varma
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons, public domain
- **Style:** Marathi academic tradition, narrative scenes with emotional depth
- **Best for:** Battle scenes, forest episodes, character studies

### Tertiary: Southeast Asian Shadow Puppets (Wayang)
- **Hundreds available**: Javanese and Balinese leather shadow puppets
- **Source:** Met Museum Open Access, Wikimedia, V&A
- **Style:** Intricate silhouettes, highly stylized, immediately recognizable
- **Best for:** L2 cluster covers, character portraits, The Leap arc

### Supplementary: Mughal/Pahari Miniatures
- **16th-19th century Indian miniatures** depicting Ramayana scenes
- **Source:** Met Museum Open Access, British Library, Wikimedia
- **Style:** Flat perspective, jewel-toned colors, garden settings
- **Best for:** The Exile and The Theft arcs, intimate scenes

### Also: Manuscript Pages
- **Mewar Ramayana** (1649-1653) — 400+ illustrated pages, many on Wikimedia
- **Kangra Ramayana** miniatures — some of the most beautiful Indian paintings ever made
- **Thai Ramakien murals** from Wat Phra Kaew (Bangkok) — spectacular but check individual image status
