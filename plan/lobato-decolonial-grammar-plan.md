# Lobato Decolonial Grammar — Plan & Ideation

## The Challenge

Monteiro Lobato (1882–1948) is the father of Brazilian children's literature. His *Sítio do Picapau Amarelo* universe is beloved across Latin America — a magical farmstead where children, a living rag doll (Emília), a corn-cob philosopher (Visconde de Sabugosa), and a Black cook (Tia Nastácia) have adventures that mix Brazilian folklore, Greek mythology, and sharp social commentary.

But Lobato was also a member of the São Paulo Eugenics Society, a personal friend of eugenicists Renato Kehl and Artur Neiva, and his works contain persistent anti-Black language — "macaca de carvão" (coal monkey), "carne preta" (black meat) — directed especially at Tia Nastácia. The 2010 CNE case identified 20+ racist passages in *Caçadas de Pedrinho* alone. The STJ case remains unresolved as of 2024.

**This grammar does not whitewash Lobato. It does not censor him either. It reads him decolonially — centering what his texts marginalized.**

## The Vision: "Sítio Reversed"

An English-language grammar that:

1. **Centers Tia Nastácia** — not as comic relief or servile foil, but as the keeper of Afro-Brazilian and Indigenous oral traditions. In *Histórias de Tia Nastácia*, Lobato accidentally created a portal: Pedrinho says "Tia Nastácia é o povo" (Tia Nastácia IS the people) and asks her to share the folklore she carries. The stories she tells — Bicho Manjaléu, Saci, the enchanted animals — are the real treasure.

2. **Surfaces the violence** — *Negrinha* is one of the most devastating short stories in Portuguese. A 7-year-old orphan, "fusca, mulatinha escura," kept as a living punching bag by Dona Inácia, a former slaveholder who misses the whip. The story is Lobato at his most self-aware — a brutal indictment of post-abolition cruelty. It belongs in any honest reading.

3. **Recovers Brazilian folklore** — O Saci (the one-legged, pipe-smoking trickster from Tupi-Guarani tradition), Cuca (the alligator-witch), Curupira (the forest protector with backwards feet) — these are Indigenous and Afro-Brazilian figures that Lobato popularized but didn't invent. The grammar honors their deeper roots.

4. **Reads Emília as resistance** — The rag doll who refuses to obey, speaks her mind, and disrupts every hierarchy is actually the most decolonial character. She mocks authority, invents her own rules, and treats Visconde (European knowledge) as useful but insufficient.

## Grammar Structure

### Grammar Name: `lobato-sitio-decolonial`
### Grammar Type: `custom`
### Language: English (translated/adapted from Portuguese sources)

### L1 Items (~60-80 items) — Three Tracks

#### Track A: "Tia Nastácia's Stories" (from *Histórias de Tia Nastácia*)
The folklore Nastácia carries — translated and retold in English. Each story is an L1 item.
- O Bicho Manjaléu
- A Princesa Ladrona
- O Sargento Verde
- João e Maria (Brazilian version)
- O Príncipe Sapo
- A Formiguinha (The Little Ant)
- O Jabuti e a Onça (Turtle and Jaguar)
- O Macaco e o Coelho (Monkey and Rabbit)
- etc. (~30 stories)

**Sections per item**: Story (English translation), Roots (Afro-Brazilian, Indigenous, or European origin), Tia Nastácia's Voice (how she introduces it), Reflection (decolonial reading note)

#### Track B: "The Violence Archive" (from *Negrinha*, *Urupês*, *Contos Completos*)
Selected short stories and passages that confront race, class, and the post-abolition wound.
- Negrinha (the full story)
- O Jardineiro Timóteo
- Bocatorta
- Urupês (the essay — Lobato's famous/infamous portrait of the "Jeca Tatu" rural caboclo)
- Negrinha's moment of joy (the doll scene — one of the most heartbreaking passages in Brazilian literature)
- Selected passages from *O Presidente Negro* (Lobato's dystopian race novel — deeply eugenicist but revealing)
- etc. (~10-15 items)

**Sections per item**: Text (English translation), Context (when/why Lobato wrote it), The Wound (what it reveals about Brazilian racial structure), Counter-Reading (scholars who've responded)

#### Track C: "The Sítio Universe" (from multiple Sítio books)
Key scenes and characters, translated, with decolonial annotations.
- Emília's Declaration of Independence (from *Memórias da Emília*)
- Pedrinho's hunt (from *Caçadas de Pedrinho* — with the racist passages visible and annotated)
- The Saci encounter (from *O Saci*)
- Visconde's science vs. Nastácia's wisdom (recurring tension)
- Dona Benta as the "benevolent" colonizer figure
- Cuca, Saci, Curupira — the folklore beings
- etc. (~15-20 items)

**Sections per item**: Scene (English translation), Characters, Decolonial Reading, Discussion Questions

### L2 Emergences (~10-15 items)

#### By Theme
- **"Tia Nastácia É o Povo"** — All the folklore stories she carries (Track A composite)
- **"The Post-Abolition Wound"** — Stories of racial violence and its aftermath (Track B composite)
- **"Emília Against the World"** — The rag doll's acts of defiance and self-invention
- **"European Knowledge at the Sítio"** — Visconde, Dona Benta, Peter Pan, Hercules — Lobato's deep engagement with European canon
- **"Indigenous Roots"** — Saci, Curupira, Cuca, and the Tupi-Guarani cosmology underneath the Sítio
- **"The Jeca Question"** — Lobato's changing view of rural Brazil (from contempt in *Urupês* to advocacy later)

#### By Character (decolonial lens)
- **Tia Nastácia** — Cook, storyteller, folk archive, target of casual cruelty
- **Emília** — Rebel, trickster, self-inventor
- **Dona Benta** — The "good" matriarch, education-as-colonization
- **Visconde de Sabugosa** — European science, literally a corn cob pretending to be a viscount
- **Pedrinho & Narizinho** — The white children who inherit the world

### L3 Meta-Categories (~3 items)
- **"What Lobato Built"** — The Sítio as national myth, its cultural power, what it teaches children
- **"What Lobato Buried"** — The racism, the eugenics, the hierarchies baked into the stories
- **"What We Can Recover"** — The Afro-Brazilian and Indigenous knowledge that lives in the margins

## Translation Approach

The source texts are in Portuguese (OCR quality: mostly readable, some artifacts). Options:

1. **AI-assisted translation** — Translate key passages with Claude, maintaining literary voice. Note: these are not public domain in English (no existing translations to use). The Portuguese originals entered Brazilian public domain in 2019 (70 years after death).
2. **Adaptive retelling** — For the folklore (Track A), retell in English rather than literal translation, since the stories themselves are oral tradition that predates Lobato.
3. **Annotated excerpts** — For Track B & C, provide translated excerpts (fair use / transformative educational purpose) with heavy annotation.

**Recommendation**: Mix of approaches. Track A = adaptive retelling (the stories are folk tradition, not "Lobato's"). Track B = annotated translation of key passages. Track C = scene summaries with translated quotes.

## Build Strategy

1. **Phase 1**: Inventory the Portuguese seeds — identify which of the 51 files contain usable text (vs. garbled OCR)
2. **Phase 2**: Extract and translate Track A (Tia Nastácia's stories) — the most self-contained and least legally fraught
3. **Phase 3**: Build Track C (Sítio scenes with decolonial annotation) — requires reading multiple source texts
4. **Phase 4**: Build Track B (violence archive) — most sensitive, needs careful scholarly framing
5. **Phase 5**: Write L2/L3 emergences with decolonial essays

## Why This Grammar Matters

Lobato is the most widely read children's author in Brazil's history. Every Brazilian child encounters the Sítio. A decolonial English grammar doesn't cancel Lobato — it completes the reading that Portuguese-language scholars like Regina Dalcastagnè and Lucilene Reginaldo have been doing. It makes that reading accessible to the English-speaking world, which knows almost nothing about this literary universe.

It also models what a "contested shelf" grammar should be: the primary text is present, the violence is visible, and the marginalized voices are centered.
