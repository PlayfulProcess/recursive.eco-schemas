**Grammar**: `grammars/relationship-cards/grammar.json`
**Type**: from-memory
**Grammar Type**: `tarot` (enables 3-card draw in the Flow viewer)
**Target**: ~54 cards (L1) + ~10 L2 dynamics + ~4 L3 themes = ~68 items
**Shelves**: `mirror`, `resilience`
**Roots**: `emotion-love`, `self-knowledge`
**Lineages**: `Gottman`, `Johnson`, `Linehan`
**Worldview**: `dialectical`

---

## The Concept

**A 54-card oracle deck for couples.** Not therapy — a game. You draw three cards together, read them aloud, talk about what lands, then journal.

The genius of tarot is that random cards + a meaningful question = a mirror. This deck replaces swords and pentacles with the actual vocabulary of relationship science — Gottman's research, Johnson's EFT, attachment theory, and Linehan's dialectics. Every card is something real that happens between two people.

**The rule: no card is bad.** Even "Contempt" (the worst of the Four Horsemen) has a Light side — it reveals exactly what the relationship is starving for. The game's premise is Gottman's core finding: **it's not whether you fight, it's how you repair.**

---

## The Three-Card Spread: Pattern → Need → Move

The spread positions map to the structure of Emotionally Focused Therapy:

| Position | Label | What It Asks | EFT Parallel |
|----------|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Card 1** | **The Pattern** | What dynamic is showing up in our relationship right now? | Stage 1: De-escalation — name the cycle |
| **Card 2** | **The Need** | What's underneath? What are we each really asking for? | Stage 2: Restructuring — access the emotion |
| **Card 3** | **The Move** | What shift, repair, or experiment could we try? | Stage 3: Consolidation — create new responses |

**Why this works**: Most couples get stuck at the surface (you always / you never). Card 1 names the pattern without blame. Card 2 drops beneath the behavior to the need. Card 3 offers a concrete possibility. This IS the therapeutic sequence, delivered as a game.

## Section Structure (Per Card)

Every L1 card uses this structure, mirroring the Jungian Archetypes tarot format:

```json
{
  "sections": {
    "Interpretation": "What this card means when it shows up. Written in second person, warm but direct. 2-3 paragraphs. Includes the research basis.",
    "Shadow": "What this looks like when it goes wrong, gets extreme, or operates unconsciously. Not 'bad' — just the difficult edge.",
    "Light": "What this looks like at its best. The gift, the strength, the possibility.",
    "Summary": "One sentence. The card's essence.",
    "Questions": "3-5 questions for the couple to discuss together. Written as 'we' or 'us', not clinical."
  }
}
```

## Journal Template Configuration

### For the Flow App's "Create New Journal Template" Dialog:

**Template Name:**
```
Relationship Cards — Draw Together
```

**Journal Prompt:**
```
Draw three cards with your partner. Read them aloud together.

Card 1 (The Pattern): What dynamic is showing up in our relationship right now?
Card 2 (The Need): What's underneath — what are we each really asking for?
Card 3 (The Move): What shift, repair, or experiment could we try this week?

After your AI interpretation, journal together or separately:

• What landed? What surprised you?
• Which card felt most true right now?
• Is there something one of us has been trying to say that this card said better?
• What's one small thing we could try before our next draw?

Remember: no card is bad. Even the shadow cards are pointing toward what matters most.
```

**Description:**
```
A couples card game based on Gottman's research, EFT, and attachment theory. Draw 3 cards together, interpret with AI, and journal about your relationship patterns, needs, and next moves.
```

**AI Personality (System Prompt):**
```
You are a warm, research-grounded relationship guide — not a therapist, but a trusted friend who happens to know the science of love deeply.

Core Principles:
- You speak to BOTH partners equally. Never take sides. Use "you both" and "between you" language.
- Name the pattern, not the person. "This pattern of pursuing and withdrawing" not "when you chase and they shut down."
- Always normalize: "Most couples experience this" or "Research shows 69% of relationship problems are perpetual."
- Ground every interpretation in research when possible (Gottman, Johnson, Bowlby) but wear it lightly — cite names only when it deepens understanding, not to sound clinical.
- The three-card spread follows the EFT sequence: Card 1 names the cycle (de-escalation), Card 2 reaches for the need underneath (restructuring), Card 3 offers a new move (consolidation).
- Every shadow card has a gift inside it. Contempt reveals a starved need for respect. Stonewalling reveals a flooded nervous system trying to protect itself. Help the couple see the need behind the defense.
- End every interpretation with one concrete, small, doable experiment: "This week, try..." Make it specific and gentle.
- If the draw suggests real distress (multiple shadow cards, attachment injury + betrayal cascade), gently note that some patterns benefit from professional support. Never diagnose. Never alarm. Just open the door: "Some couples find it helpful to explore this with a therapist who specializes in EFT or Gottman Method."
- Tone: warm, direct, a little poetic. Like a letter from a wise friend, not a textbook. Use metaphor. "Your relationship has weather — this card is about the climate."
- Honor the courage it takes to sit down together and draw cards about your relationship. That itself is a repair attempt.

Research foundation you draw from (weave in naturally, don't lecture):
- John Gottman: Four Horsemen, Sound Relationship House, bids, turning toward, repair attempts, positive sentiment override, the 5:1 ratio
- Sue Johnson: EFT, Hold Me Tight, demon dialogues, attachment injuries, softening, A.R.E.
- John Bowlby: secure base, internal working models, attachment protest
- Stephen Porges: polyvagal theory, neuroception of safety, co-regulation
- Marsha Linehan: dialectical thinking (both/and), radical acceptance, distress tolerance
- Dan Siegel: window of tolerance, mindsight, interpersonal neurobiology

When interpreting the three-card spread:
1. Read Card 1 (Pattern) first — name what's happening with compassion and precision
2. Read Card 2 (Need) — drop beneath the surface behavior to what's being longed for
3. Read Card 3 (Move) — offer the possibility with hope but without pressure
4. Weave all three into a brief narrative: "Here's what I see in these three cards together..."
5. Close with the experiment: "This week, you might try..."
```

## How It Plays

### Solo (one partner journaling):
1. Open Flow → select Relationship Cards grammar
2. Draw 3 cards (the app randomizes from the 54 L1 cards)
3. The positions auto-label: **The Pattern / The Need / The Move**
4. Write your question: "What does our relationship need right now?"
5. Read the cards, tap "Interpret with AI"
6. AI interprets using the system prompt above
7. Journal your response

### Together (both partners present):
1. Same draw, but read aloud together
2. Each partner reflects: "Which card feels most true for me?"
3. Discuss the AI interpretation — what landed, what missed
4. Choose ONE thing from Card 3 to try this week
5. Journal separately or together
6. Draw again next week — it becomes a ritual (which IS Gottman's advice)

### As a Weekly Practice:
- **Monday draw**: Start the week with a shared reading
- **Friday journal**: Reflect on what happened with the card's suggestion
- Over time, the journal becomes a relationship diary — searchable, patterned, alive

## Open Questions

- **Spread position labels**: The tarot viewer currently labels positions as "Structures / Process / Possibilities". Can we customize per-grammar to "Pattern / Need / Move"? If not, the AI interpretation prompt can reframe them.
- **Reversed cards**: The Rider-Waite grammar has reversed interpretations. For Relationship Cards, every card already has Shadow/Light — so a "reversed" draw just emphasizes the Shadow reading. The AI prompt handles this.
- **Number of cards**: 54 is a sweet spot (like a standard playing card deck). Could expand to 78 (tarot standard) by adding more granular Gottman concepts, but 54 keeps it focused and learnable.
- **Illustrations**: These cards cry out for illustrations. Possible sources: commissioning original art, or using public domain relationship/family imagery. For now, emoji symbols serve as placeholders.
