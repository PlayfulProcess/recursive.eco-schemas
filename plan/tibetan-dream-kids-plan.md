# The Lands of Sleep: A Dream Guide for Bedtime — Design Plan

**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Grammar slug:** `tibetan-dream-kids`
**Grammar type:** `custom`
**Approach:** From memory, rhyming verse, esoteric/animist/cosmic lens
**Format:** Same series: 3 stanzas, AABB/ABAB, ALL CAPS keywords, 36 L1 stories (7+6+6+6+6+5)
**Special:** The ONLY grammar designed to be read AS a bedtime ritual — prospective, not retrospective

---

## Vision

The Bardo Thodol (Tibetan Book of the Dead) reframed as a children's dream guide. The six bardos become six stages of a night's sleep. The peaceful and wrathful deities become the gentle and scary things you meet in dreams. Lucid dreaming becomes the recognition that you're dreaming — the moment of liberation. Waking up is rebirth.

This grammar is designed to be read TO a child who is falling asleep. The first arc ("The Threshold") accompanies the actual process of falling asleep. The middle arcs prepare the child for whatever dreams may come. The last arc ("The Dawn") is for morning — to be read (or remembered) upon waking.

Esoteric sources:
- **Padmasambhava / Karma Lingpa** — The Bardo Thodol (Liberation Through Hearing During the Intermediate State)
- **Tenzin Wangyal Rinpoche** — The Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep (the six practices of dream yoga)
- **Stephen LaBerge** — Lucid dreaming research (Western science meeting Tibetan practice)
- **Carl Jung** — Active imagination, the collective unconscious, the archetypes encountered in dreams
- **Rudolf Steiner** — Sleep as the soul's journey through spiritual worlds each night

## Reading Instructions (Unique to This Grammar)

This grammar is designed for a specific reading ritual:

1. **At bedtime**: Read 1-2 stories from "The Threshold" as the child settles in
2. **As eyes close**: Read one story from "The Peaceful Lands" or "The Wrathful Guardians" (parent's choice based on the child's current emotional state)
3. **If the child had nightmares recently**: Read from "The Wrathful Guardians" — these stories reframe nightmares as protectors
4. **If the child is interested in lucid dreaming**: Read from "The Mirror" arc
5. **In the morning**: Read from "The Dawn" arc to help the child recall and process their dreams
6. **"The Deep" stories**: Read these on quiet nights when the child is curious about what happens when we DON'T dream

## Illustration Sources

### Primary: Tibetan Thangka Paintings
- Peaceful and wrathful deity thangkas from museum collections (Met, Rubin Museum of Art)
- Public domain / open access where available
- **Style:** Luminous colors, mandala structure, peaceful deities in soft gold/blue, wrathful deities in rich red/black

### Secondary: Odilon Redon (1840-1916)
- French Symbolist — his pastels are the most dream-like art in Western tradition
- Public domain
- **Style:** Floating figures, impossible colors, dream-logic compositions
- **Best for:** The Peaceful Lands, The Mirror

### Tertiary: Paul Klee (1879-1940)
- Some works in public domain (pre-1924 US publication)
- **Style:** Childlike geometry, color-as-mood, imaginary landscapes
- **Best for:** The Threshold, The Dawn

### Supplementary: Hilma af Klint (1862-1944)
- Swedish mystic painter — some works now public domain
- **Style:** Abstract spiritual visions, mandala-like, luminous
- **Best for:** The Deep, The Mirror
