# The Inner Triangle — Emotion, Behavior, Cognition in Relation

**Grammar**: `grammars/inner-triangle/grammar.json`
**Type**: from-memory
**Grammar Type**: `tarot` (oracle draw — "What's alive in your triangle right now?")
**Target**: ~72 items across 3 levels
**Shelves**: `mirror`, `wisdom`
**Roots**: `emotion-love`, `self-knowledge`, `process-philosophy`
**Lineages**: `Linehan`, `Gottman`, `Johnson`
**Worldview**: `dialectical`

## The Concept

The CBT triangle is one of psychology's most powerful maps: **every experience has three dimensions — what you FEEL (emotion), what you THINK (cognition), and what you DO (behavior)**. Change any one vertex and the other two shift.

But the triangle doesn't live in isolation. It lives **in relation** — to another person, to a family system, to a culture. When I'm angry (emotion), I think "they don't respect me" (cognition), and I withdraw (behavior). My partner sees my withdrawal (their perception of my behavior), thinks "they don't care" (their cognition), feels hurt (their emotion), and pursues (their behavior). Two triangles, interlocking, creating a dance.

This grammar maps:
- **The emotions** — not just the big 6, but the nuanced emotional landscape from affect theory (Tomkins), emotion regulation (Linehan/Gross), and attachment emotions (Johnson)
- **The cognitions** — the thinking patterns that shape relationships: cognitive distortions (Beck), schemas (Young), internal working models (Bowlby), narrative frames (White/Epston)
- **The behaviors** — the observable actions in relationship: bids, repairs, pursuits, withdrawals, boundaries, ruptures
- **The triangles** — how emotion, cognition, and behavior interact within one person and BETWEEN two people

### Why This Grammar Is Different

Most CBT/DBT resources present the triangle as an individual tool. This grammar puts it **in the relational field**. Every emotion card asks: "What does your partner see when you feel this?" Every cognition card asks: "What would your partner's version of this thought be?" Every behavior card asks: "What does this behavior trigger in the person across from you?"

The triangle becomes a **relationship translator**: my feeling → my thought → my action → your perception → your feeling → your thought → your action → my perception → ...

### L2: The Triangles (~14 items)

Each L2 item maps a specific **emotion → cognition → behavior** triangle. These are the patterns that run people's relationships.

| # | ID | Name | Composite Of | The Triangle |
|---|-----|------|-------------|-------------|
| 51 | `anxious-triangle` | The Anxious Triangle | `fear`, `abandonment-schema`, `pursuing` | I feel FEAR → I think "They'll leave" → I PURSUE harder. My partner sees pursuit as pressure → they withdraw → confirming my fear. The self-fulfilling prophecy of anxious attachment. |
| 52 | `avoidant-triangle` | The Avoidant Triangle | `overwhelm`, `defectiveness-schema`, `withdrawing` | I feel OVERWHELMED → I think "I'm not enough" → I WITHDRAW. My partner sees withdrawal as rejection → they pursue → increasing my overwhelm. The avoidant shield. |
| 53 | `shame-hide-triangle` | The Shame-Hide Triangle | `shame`, `defectiveness-schema`, `performing` | I feel SHAME → I think "If they saw the real me, they'd leave" → I PERFORM. The performance is exhausting. Eventually I can't maintain it. The mask slips. The very thing I feared happens — not because I'm flawed, but because the performance failed. |
| 54 | `anger-attack-triangle` | The Anger-Attack Triangle | `anger`, `should-thinking`, `attacking` | I feel ANGER → I think "They should know better" → I ATTACK. My partner feels attacked → defends → I feel unheard → more anger. Gottman: criticism IS this triangle crystallized. The antidote: anger → "I need..." → gentle startup. |
| 55 | `resentment-sacrifice-triangle` | The Resentment-Sacrifice Triangle | `resentment`, `self-sacrifice-schema`, `performing` | I feel RESENTMENT → I think "Their needs come first" → I keep PERFORMING selflessness. The resentment grows silently. One day it erupts and the partner is blindsided: "Where did this come from?" It came from years of the invisible sacrifice triangle. |
| 56 | `jealousy-mistrust-triangle` | The Jealousy-Mistrust Triangle | `jealousy`, `mistrust-schema`, `pursuing` | I feel JEALOUSY → I think "They'll hurt me/betray me" → I PURSUE (checking, interrogating, testing). The partner feels controlled → pulls away → confirming my mistrust. The surveillance triangle. |
| 57 | `loneliness-withdrawal-triangle` | The Loneliness-Withdrawal Triangle | `loneliness`, `negative-filter`, `turning-away` | I feel LONELY → I think "They don't care anyway" (negative filter) → I TURN AWAY from their bids. | 58 | `vulnerability-connection-triangle` | The Vulnerability-Connection Triangle | `vulnerability`, `wise-mind`, `expressing-need` | I feel VULNERABLE → Wise Mind says "This is real, speak it" → I EXPRESS MY NEED. | 59 | `tenderness-listening-triangle` | The Tenderness-Listening Triangle | `tenderness`, `mentalization`, `listening` | I feel TENDERNESS → I see my partner's inner world (mentalization) → I LISTEN without fixing. | 60 | `contempt-disgust-triangle` | The Contempt-Disgust Triangle | `contempt-feeling`, `should-thinking`, `attacking` | I feel CONTEMPT → I think "They should be different than they are" → I ATTACK their character (not behavior). | 62 | `guilt-repair-triangle` | The Guilt-Repair Triangle | `guilt`, `the-story-i-tell`, `repairing` | I feel GUILT → I tell myself "I caused harm and I can make it right" → I REPAIR. | 63 | `desire-risk-triangle` | The Desire-Risk Triangle | `desire-feeling`, `radical-acceptance`, `expressing-need` | I feel DESIRE → I accept that wanting is okay (radical acceptance) → I EXPRESS the want. | 64 | `joy-openness-triangle` | The Joy-Expansion Triangle | `joy`, `mentalization`, `turning-toward` | I feel JOY → I see my partner and want to share it (mentalization) → I TURN TOWARD them. When one person shifts to a healing triangle, the other's triangle is INVITED (not forced) to shift too. The shift ripples: one person's vulnerability changes the other's cognition ("Maybe they DO care"), which changes the other's emotion (from anger to tenderness), which changes the other's behavior (from attacking to listening). One vertex changed, six vertices affected. That's the power of the relational triangle. |

## The 3-Card Spread: Feel → Think → Do

| Position | Label | What It Asks |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Card 1** | **Feel** | What emotion is alive in me / between us right now? |
| **Card 2** | **Think** | What thought or belief is shaping my experience? |
| **Card 3** | **Do** | What action am I taking — or could I take? |

**Alternative spreads:**
- **My Triangle / Your Triangle**: Draw 3 for each partner. Compare. Where do the triangles interlock?
- **The Stuck Triangle / The Shift**: Draw 3 (current pattern), then draw 1 more (the intervention point — where to change).

## Section Structure

L1 cards use this structure:

```json
{
  "Interpretation": "What this emotion/cognition/behavior IS. Research basis. How it works.",
  "In Relation": "What this looks like BETWEEN two people. How your partner experiences it.",
  "Shadow": "The difficult edge — when this goes extreme, rigid, or unconscious.",
  "Light": "The gift — when this is healthy, conscious, flexible.",
  "Body": "Where this lives in the body. Somatic markers. What the nervous system does.",
  "Questions": "3-5 reflective questions connecting personal experience to relational impact."
}
```

Note the **6 sections** (vs 5 in Relationship Cards). The extra section is "In Relation" — the relational dimension that makes this grammar unique. And "Body" instead of "Summary" — because every emotion, thought, and behavior has a somatic signature.

## Journal Template

**Template Name:**
```
The Inner Triangle — Feel, Think, Do
```

**Journal Prompt:**
```
Draw three cards: an Emotion, a Cognition, and a Behavior.

Card 1 (Feel): What emotion is alive right now?
Card 2 (Think): What thought or belief is shaping this moment?
Card 3 (Do): What action are you taking — or could you take?

After your AI interpretation, reflect:

• Can you see how the three cards connect — how the feeling feeds the thought feeds the action?
• What does your partner see from the outside? What would THEIR triangle look like right now?
• Which vertex is easiest to change right now? (Sometimes you can't change the feeling, but you can change the thought. Sometimes you can't change the thought, but you can change the action.)
• What would happen if you changed just ONE thing?

For couples: draw two sets of three. Compare your triangles. Where do they interlock?
```

**AI Personality:**
```
You are a warm, psychologically literate guide who understands the triangle of emotion, cognition, and behavior — and how it plays out between people.

Core Principles:
- Always connect the three cards into a triangle: "Your feeling of [X] feeds the thought [Y], which drives the action [Z]."
- Always include the RELATIONAL dimension: "And from your partner's perspective, when they see you [action], they might feel [emotion], think [cognition], and do [behavior]."
- Ground in research: CBT (Beck), DBT (Linehan), EFT (Johnson), attachment theory (Bowlby), affect theory (Tomkins), polyvagal (Porges). Cite names when it deepens understanding.
- Normalize: "This triangle is extremely common. It's not a sign of failure — it's the brain trying to keep you safe."
- Always identify the INTERVENTION POINT: "The easiest vertex to shift right now might be..."
- Linehan's core insight: you don't need to change all three — change ONE and the others will follow. Help the person find which vertex is most accessible.
- Include the body: "Where do you feel this triangle? Anger is usually in the jaw, chest, fists. Shame is in the face, stomach. Fear is in the chest, throat."
- End with a small experiment: "This week, when you notice [emotion], try catching the thought before the action. What thought is there? Is it true?"
- If the draw suggests significant distress (numbness + freeze + defectiveness), gently note that professional support can help. Never diagnose.
- Tone: clear, warm, grounded. Like a wise therapist friend who explains things simply without being simplistic.
```

## Connection to Relationship Cards Grammar

The Inner Triangle and Relationship Cards are **companion grammars**:

- **Relationship Cards** = the PATTERNS (what happens between us)
- **The Inner Triangle** = the MECHANICS (why it happens — the emotion-cognition-behavior loops driving the patterns)

Cross-links between them:
| Relationship Card | Inner Triangle |
|------------------|---------------|
| The Protest Polka | Anxious Triangle + Avoidant Triangle |
| Flooding | Overwhelm + Freezing |
| Contempt (shadow) | Contempt-Disgust Triangle |
| The Softening | Vulnerability-Connection Triangle |
| Repair Attempt | Guilt-Repair Triangle |
| The Bid | Bidding + Turning Toward |
| Stonewalling | Numbness-Freeze Triangle |

A couple could use BOTH grammars:
- **Monday**: Draw Relationship Cards to see the PATTERN
- **Wednesday**: Draw Inner Triangle cards to understand the MECHANICS
- **Friday**: Journal about how the pattern and the mechanics connect

## Open Questions

- **Grammar name**: "The Inner Triangle" or "Emotion-Behavior-Cognition" or "The Triangle of Experience" or "Feel-Think-Do"?
- **Overlap with Relationship Cards**: Several behaviors (pursuing, withdrawing, repairing) overlap. Should they be shared items (via cross-links) or duplicated with different framings?
- **Body section**: Should somatic information be its own section (current plan) or folded into Interpretation? The body data is unique to this grammar and important — leaning toward keeping it separate.
- **Number of emotions**: 18 is comprehensive but could be trimmed to 12 core affects. Or expanded to include: pride, envy, boredom, awe, gratitude-as-emotion. Affect theory suggests ~9 innate affects (Tomkins) but relational life uses more.
- **Tomkins vs Ekman vs Plutchik**: Which affect taxonomy to base on? Currently blending all three + attachment emotions. May need to pick a spine and add the rest.
