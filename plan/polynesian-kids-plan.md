# The Ocean's Children (polynesian-kids) — Design Plan

**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Grammar slug:** `polynesian-kids`
**Series:** *Stories Before Sleep* (Bedtime Mythology Series)
**Tradition:** Polynesian (Māori, Hawaiian, Samoan, Tongan — pan-Polynesian shared stories)
**Format:** Same series: 3 stanzas, AABB/ABAB, ALL CAPS keywords, 42 L1 stories (7 per arc × 6 arcs)

---

## The Vision

The OCEAN as a living being. Navigation as spiritual practice. Maui as the ultimate trickster. Pele as the creative/destructive goddess. The voyaging canoe as living technology. The concept of mana (spiritual power flowing through all things).

This grammar tells the Polynesian story from separation to settlement — from the moment Rangi and Papa were torn apart to the arrival of the great fleet in Aotearoa. The ocean is not a barrier but a CHARACTER. The navigator doesn't move through space; space moves around the navigator.

**Esoteric lens:** Stars as ancestors. The ocean as conscious. Navigation as meditation. Islands as living beings fished up from the deep. The voyaging canoe as a model of community — everyone paddles, everyone matters.

**Unique gift to the series:** NAVIGATION as sacred practice (unique among world mythologies), the ocean as a character, Maui as the ultimate trickster (makes Homer's Odysseus look timid), mana as spiritual power, the voyaging canoe as living technology.

## Metadata Fields (per L1 item)

- `polynesian_source`: Which tradition(s) the story comes from (Māori, Hawaiian, Samoan, etc.)
- `archetype`: Campbell parallel
- `pop_culture`: Modern reference (Moana, etc.)

## Illustration Sources

- **Primary:** Tapa cloth designs and petroglyphs — bold geometric patterns
- **Secondary:** Early European contact illustrations (Cook expedition artists: William Hodges, John Webber — public domain)
- **Tertiary:** Hawaiian and Māori carving photographs (museum open access)
- **Note:** Careful with sacred images — use artistic/decorative tradition, not sacred carved forms without cultural guidance

## Cultural Sensitivity

- Use widely-shared pan-Polynesian stories (Maui cycle, Rangi and Papa) rather than culturally specific sacred narratives
- The navigation tradition is shared and celebrated
- Credit specific traditions where stories differ across islands
- Whisper sections point to Polynesian voices and acknowledge protocols
