## Writing Approach

- Combines Iliad and Odyssey into a single grammar (unlike most treatments that separate them)
- The six arcs form a complete narrative: war → heroes → gods → voyage → homecoming → echo
- Simone Weil's "The Iliad, or the Poem of Force" as key interpretive lens for Wrath arc
- Campbell's monomyth most explicit here — the Odyssey IS the hero's journey
- Shamanic immram (Otherworld voyage) parallel for the Voyage arc
- Wine-dark sea (oinops pontos) as recurring poetic motif

## Learnings

1. **Homer combines naturally**: The Iliad and Odyssey work better together than apart for kids. The Iliad gives you the war; the Odyssey gives you the adventure. The Echo arc bridges them through aftermath stories (Troy burns → Aeneas flees → Telemachus seeks).

2. **The Voyage arc has 8 stories**: It's the longest because the Odyssey's episodic structure gives you 8 distinct adventures. Every other arc has 7. The asymmetry works — the Voyage IS the long, wandering part.

3. **L2 thematic groupings**: The L2 clusters group stories by dramatic function rather than strict sequence:
   - "How the War Began" / "The Rage of Achilles" / "The Human Cost" (Wrath)
   - "Monsters and Enchantresses" / "Temptations of Forgetting" / "Almost Home" (Voyage)
   - "The Testing of the King" / "The Reckoning" / "The Reunion" (Homecoming)

4. **Star Wars parallels are strongest here**: Lucas explicitly based Star Wars on Campbell's Hero with a Thousand Faces, which draws heavily on the Odyssey. The L3 Star Wars sections practically write themselves.

5. **17 L2 clusters (not the planned ~22)**: The material grouped more tightly than expected. The Echo arc only needs 2 clusters. This matches the Arthurian grammar's pattern of ~3 clusters per arc.
