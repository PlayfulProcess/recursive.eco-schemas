## Root System

These stories are original contemporary fiction. No cultural material is quoted, retold, or adapted in the story text itself. The wisdom that shapes them comes from the following sources, acknowledged here with gratitude.

### Noel Nannup (Noongar Elder)

The deepest root. Elder Nannup describes two gifts given to Aboriginal children:

1. **Shadow awareness** — from the earliest age, children are taught to be physically aware of where their shadow falls. By age seven, a child can run at full speed through a group of people without casting their shadow on anyone. "What you're doing is you're looking where everyone else is. The last person you're thinking about is yourself."

2. **"I have everything I need"** — your aunties, instructed by your great-great-grandmother, find an aromatic plant and place its seeds above where your placenta is buried. The tree grows. Its fine feeder roots draw nutrients from the placenta. Your DNA is now in the tree. "You cannot have a closer relationship with something in the natural world than for it to carry your same DNA."

These are not two separate teachings. They are one gesture: radical awareness of others AND radical belonging to place, held together. Each makes the other possible.

Elder Nannup also speaks of law embedded in land — "the law is determined by the spirit of the place" — and of somatic accountability so deep that violating law causes physical collapse. "You within yourself become the judge and the jury."

Source: The Emerald podcast. Also references Keith Basso's work on wisdom sitting in places.

### Marsha Linehan (DBT / Dialectical Behavior Therapy)

The dialectical frame: two things that seem contradictory can both be true. "You didn't cause this AND you are the only one who can fix it." Radical acceptance — not approval of what is, but the refusal to add suffering to suffering by fighting reality.

Season 1's shadow-awareness stories (asking > complaining, thanking > praising) are interpersonal effectiveness and mindfulness in Linehan's framework. Season 2's arrival stories are radical acceptance — the moment when trying to be better becomes its own obstacle.

### Chuang Tzu

The useless tree that survives because it serves no one's purpose. Wu wei — non-doing, effortless action. "The Tao is in the ant, in the tile, in the dung." The butterfly dream. Freedom as what remains when you stop reaching for it.

Story 6 (Being > Performing) carries this directly: Shiryo becomes so good at the techniques that the technique itself becomes the obstacle.

### The Emerald Podcast (Josh Shrei)

The broader context for all of this — re-enchantment, animism as lived practice, the somatic intelligence that precedes and exceeds conceptual knowing. The podcast episode featuring Noel Nannup is the direct source, but the entire project of treating story as technology (not entertainment) comes from this lineage.

## What These Stories Are Not

- They are not retellings of Aboriginal stories
- They are not adaptations of Noongar ceremony or law
- They do not claim to represent any indigenous tradition
- They do not use indigenous words, characters, or settings

They are a copper robot, a cat, and a parent in a kitchen. The wisdom is in the soil. What grows above ground is entirely its own.

---

## The Recursive Signature

Every story in Season 1 ends: *"Not because they knew the answer. But because they were learning too."*

Story 8 — the final story — breaks this pattern. The signature disappears. Not because the learning stops, but because it has become so continuous it no longer needs naming.

This structural choice is the stories' own. It is not borrowed from anywhere.
