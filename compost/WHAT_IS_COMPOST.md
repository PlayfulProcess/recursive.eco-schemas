# What Is Compost?

> "The master's tools will never dismantle the master's house." — Audre Lorde
> "But they can be composted." — the garden

## The Principle

Compost is any text, system, or grammar that **can be used to further colonialism**.

This does not mean everything in the compost pile is bad. A brilliant novel can carry colonial assumptions. A children's classic can reproduce racial hierarchies. A philosophical masterpiece can naturalize domination. The quality of the writing is not the question. The question is: **what does this feed?**

Compost is not cancellation. Cancellation pretends the thing never existed. Compost acknowledges that it existed, that it shaped the soil we're standing on, and that it needs to be broken down before it can nourish anything new.

## How Composting Works

In a garden, compost is organic matter that decomposes. The heat of decomposition kills pathogens. What remains feeds new growth. The original form is unrecognizable — and that's the point.

In this library:

1. **The raw material goes in**: The original texts, with their colonial assumptions intact. We don't sanitize them. You can't compost what you refuse to touch.

2. **The decomposers do their work**: Decolonial thinkers, counter-narratives, the voices that were marginalized by the original text. Du Bois decomposes Gobineau. Achebe decomposes Conrad. Nastácia decomposes Lobato.

3. **New growth emerges**: Grammars that retell, reframe, recenter. Not the original text cleaned up — something genuinely new, grown from what was broken down.

## What Goes in the Compost

- Texts that naturalize racial hierarchy (even beautifully written ones)
- Children's literature that teaches children who matters and who doesn't
- Philosophical systems that universalize one culture's perspective as "reason"
- Spiritual systems that encode racial evolution as cosmic law
- Any text whose unexamined assumptions can be — and have been — weaponized

## What Compost Is NOT

- **Not a ban list**: Everything here can be read. Should be read. The point is HOW you read it.
- **Not a judgment of readers**: If you loved these books as a child, that's part of the story too. Compost doesn't shame — it transforms.
- **Not a claim that the authors had no genius**: Lobato was a genius. Kipling was a genius. Genius and colonialism are not mutually exclusive — that's precisely what makes colonial texts so effective.
- **Not only for white/European texts**: Colonialism lives in every culture that was touched by it, including the cultures that resisted it. Internalized hierarchies need composting too.

## The Gesture

A Yoruba descendant in colonial Brazil goes to church on Sunday. She kneels. She bows her head — *bate a cabeça*.

The colonizers see submission. A Christian convert. Another soul saved.

But beneath the church floor, her gods are buried. She placed them there herself. Every time she bows, she is not reverencing the colonial idol above — she is reciprocating with the animist forces below. She found a way to keep faith with her ancestors inside the master's house.

That is composting.

The structure looks colonial. The gesture is something else entirely. The form survives because it learned to live inside what tried to kill it.

This folder exists in that spirit.

## For the Children

The simplest way to explain compost to a child:

*Some old stories have beautiful parts and hurtful parts mixed together. We don't throw them away and we don't pretend the hurtful parts aren't there. We put them in the compost pile — where they break down slowly, and the good soil that's left helps new stories grow. The new stories remember what happened, but they belong to everyone.*
